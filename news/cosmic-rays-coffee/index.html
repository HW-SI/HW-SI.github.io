Could Cosmic Rays Power My Coffee Machine?

2 May 2026 | Group update blog | Back-of-the-envelope astrophysics

What are cosmic rays?

Cosmic rays are tiny particles arriving from space. Despite the name, they are not rays like sunlight. Most are charged particles: mainly protons and atomic nuclei, with some electrons. They travel extremely fast, often close to the speed of light. NASA describes them as high-energy particles and one of our few direct samples of matter from outside the Solar System.

Their origins are still studied. Some come from the Sun, many from within our Galaxy, and some may be accelerated in violent environments such as supernova remnants, compact objects, or black-hole-related systems. Because they are charged, magnetic fields bend their paths, so they usually do not point directly back to their sources.

When cosmic rays reach Earth, they often hit atoms high in the atmosphere and create showers of secondary particles. So cosmic rays are all around us, but they arrive as individual particles, not as a smooth beam of usable energy.

The simple estimate

Let us use a generous estimate.

Around 1 GeV, the Pierre Auger Observatory gives a cosmic-ray rate of about:

10,000 particles per square metre per second

At higher energies, the rate drops quickly. For example, at 1000 GeV, only about one particle arrives per square metre per second.

For simple arithmetic, suppose each particle has about:

3 GeV ~= 0.0000000005 joule

Now multiply:

10,000 particles per second
x 0.0000000005 joule per particle
= 0.000005 watts per square metre

So even a perfect collector receives only:

0.000005 watts per square metre

That is five millionths of a watt.

A useful way to remember this is:

1 square kilometre = 1,000,000 square metres

0.000005 watts x 1,000,000
= 5 watts per square kilometre

So cosmic rays provide roughly:

5 watts per square kilometre

under this generous estimate.

Now compare it with a coffee machine

A common home coffee maker uses roughly 600-1500 watts, so 1000 watts is a reasonable round number.

If one square kilometre of perfect cosmic-ray collector gives about 5 watts, then the area needed for one coffee machine is:

1000 watts / 5 watts per square kilometre
= 200 square kilometres

That is roughly a square about:

14 km x 14 km

So to power one coffee machine, we would need a perfect cosmic-ray collector covering an area larger than many cities.

And this is still unrealistic. We assumed that the collector sits above the atmosphere, catches every cosmic ray, and converts all particle energy into electricity. A real device would do much worse.

The lesson is clear: cosmic rays are not a practical power source. They are far too sparse.

What if we used the whole Earth?

Now scale up.

Earth's surface area is about:

510 million square kilometres

Using our generous estimate:

510 million square kilometres
x 5 watts per square kilometre
~= 2.5 billion watts

That is:

2.5 gigawatts

This sounds large. A few gigawatts is comparable to the output of a few large power plants.

But remember: this assumes the entire Earth is a perfect cosmic-ray collector. No atmosphere losses, no engineering losses, and perfect energy conversion. Even then, the number is only a few gigawatts.

Compare with human energy use

Humanity uses much more energy.

The Energy Institute reports that global energy demand reached about 592 exajoules in 2024. Spread over one year, that corresponds to an average power of roughly:

19 trillion watts

or:

19 terawatts

Now compare:

Cosmic rays received by Earth:   about 2.5 gigawatts
Human energy use:                about 19,000 gigawatts

So humanity uses roughly:

19,000 / 2.5 ~= 8000

times more power than the total cosmic-ray power reaching Earth in this generous estimate.

Even if Earth were a perfect cosmic-ray power station, cosmic rays would still fall thousands of times short of human energy use.

So why do we care about cosmic rays?

Cosmic rays are not useful because they can heat water. They are useful because they carry information.

They tell us that somewhere in the Universe, nature is accelerating particles to enormous speeds. They point us toward extreme environments: exploding stars, strong magnetic fields, shocks, compact objects, and possibly black-hole jets. They connect naturally to gamma rays, neutrinos, and multi-messenger astrophysics.

So the answer to the title question is simple:

No, cosmic rays cannot power my coffee machine.

But the calculation is still worth doing. It shows that the Universe can send us particles that are individually energetic but collectively faint.

Cosmic rays are not a fuel. They are messengers. And for astrophysics, that is much more interesting.
